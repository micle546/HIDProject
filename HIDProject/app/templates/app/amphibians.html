Amphibians

Amphibians are vertebrates, meaning they have a spine, that have skin that they can absorb water and gasses through from their environment. They are semi-aquatic, and generally live near a water source so that they can keep their skin moist.

Since their skin is designed to let things through into their bodies, amphibians are especially susceptible to toxins or other contaminants in the environment.

Amphibians lay eggs, and most amphibians have a larval stage in their life cycles. This means they go through some form of metamorphosis where they change from one shape to another while growing up. These larval stages are usually aquatic and generally have gills to breathe in the water. Adult stages usually do not keep their gills and instead breathe with lungs.

Common amphibians include frogs, toads, newts, and salamanders.
